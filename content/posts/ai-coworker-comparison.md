## What Is an AI Coworker?

An AI coworker isn't a chatbot you open in a separate tab. It's an autonomous agent that lives inside the tools you already use — your messaging platform, your meeting software, your project management system — and **executes work** without waiting for a prompt.

The key difference from older AI tools is *proactivity*. A chatbot waits for input. An AI coworker observes, suggests, and acts. It spots a bug in your Slack thread and opens a Jira ticket. It hears a customer mention a feature request in a meeting and drafts the spec. It sees you're walking past a grocery store and sends your shopping list.

The category exploded in June 2026 because three things converged:

1. **Persistent context** — AI models can now maintain coherent memory across days or weeks, not just a single chat session.
2. **Tool integration maturity** — Connecting to thousands of third-party APIs (Slack, Jira, HubSpot, Salesforce, Google Workspace, Stripe) is table stakes now.
3. **Proactive execution** — Instead of "ask and answer," these agents run condition-based watchers and scheduled automations that fire without human initiation.

The result? An AI coworker comparison isn't about which chatbot has better answers. It's about which agent reliably ships work.

## Step-by-Step: How to Choose Your AI Coworker

Choosing between Mina, Viktor, and folk isn't about features — it's about **where your work actually happens**. Here's how to evaluate each one for your specific situation.

### Step 1: Map your primary work surface

Where do you spend most of your working hours?

- **Meetings (4-8 hours/day):** Mina is your candidate. It joins Zoom, Google Meet, and Teams calls as an active participant. You don't get a transcript afterward — Mina speaks *during* the meeting to answer questions, pull context, and create deliverables.
- **Slack/chat as your office:** Viktor is built for this. It lives in your Slack workspace, watches channels and threads, and proactively executes tasks — managing campaigns, writing code, delivering reports, building apps.
- **Personal messaging (iMessage, Telegram, Discord):** folk is designed for this. It operates in your text message threads, knows your location, plans your schedule, and coordinates with other friends' folk agents.

Most people will find one of these three dominates their workflow. Pick the one that matches your primary surface.

### Step 2: Define the kind of work you want to delegate

The three agents handle different task types:

**Mina handles:**
- Real-time Q&A during calls ("What's our Q2 pipeline number?")
- CRM updates while the conversation happens
- Drafting proposals, summaries, follow-ups during the meeting
- Function-specific roles: AI Sales Engineer, AI Scrum Master, AI Recruitment coordinator, AI Customer Success manager

**Viktor handles:**
- Ad campaign management (Meta & Google Ads from Slack)
- Code writing and deployment
- Report generation and dashboard creation
- Automated workflow creation based on observed team patterns
- Media buying and bid management

**folk handles:**
- Personal scheduling and date planning
- Location-based reminders (grocery store proximity, flight tracking)
- Research tasks sent via text
- Multi-agent coordination ("Ask Maya's folk to find a sushi spot Friday")
- Browser-based actions with approval gates

### Step 3: Evaluate the setup investment

| Agent | Time to first result | Setup complexity |

| **Viktor** | ~1 hour | Install Slack app, connect integrations, set up skills |

folk is fastest — you just text it. Viktor takes the longest because you're setting up a team AI that needs access to your full tool stack.

## FAQ

### Can I use more than one AI coworker at the same time?

Yes. These agents cover different surfaces, so they're complementary rather than competitive. Many teams use Mina in meetings and Viktor in Slack. Just watch your total monthly spend — two $49-50 subscriptions add up.

### Are AI coworkers secure enough for enterprise use?

It depends on the agent. Mina uses secure OAuth and never deletes data without user approval. Viktor's SOC 2 Type 2 and ISO 27001 certifications are still in progress. folk operates on a per-user isolated knowledge graph and does not store payment card details, but has no enterprise compliance certs yet.

### What happens if an AI coworker makes a mistake?

Mina and Viktor both take action autonomously, but with different safety models. Viktor's Skills Marketplace allows pre-vetted actions, and users report that it "did everything correctly the first time" — but some reviewers want more visibility into *why* it took certain actions. folk uses approval gates for high-risk actions like placing orders (stops at the pay step).

### Can I train an AI coworker on my specific workflows?

Yes, all three support customization. Mina uses "function-aligned agents" — pre-built roles (Sales Engineer, Scrum Master, Recruitment) that you deploy and customize. Viktor offers a Skills Marketplace with pre-built skills and lets you create custom automations based on how your team actually works. folk supports custom plugins and MCP servers — just ask folk in chat to connect something new.

### Which AI coworker is best for a solo operator?

For solo operators, the answer depends on your primary surface. If you live in Slack, Viktor (at $15/user/month) is cost-effective. If you attend frequent client meetings, Mina's free tier covers substantial evaluation. If you want a personal assistant that texts you, folk's free tier and low setup time make it the fastest path to value.
