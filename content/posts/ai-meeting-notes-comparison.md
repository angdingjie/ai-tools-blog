You're in back-to-back meetings all day. Someone says something important at minute 23. Your hand-written notes are a mess. You spend 15 minutes after each call typing up action items — or worse, you forget them entirely.

AI meeting notes tools promise to fix this. But with six major contenders — Fireflies, Otter, Fathom, Granola, Read AI, and Plaud — all claiming to be the best, picking the right one is its own decision to make. Spend too long evaluating them, and you've wasted the time you were trying to save.

Here's the reality: **these tools differ radically in approach, not just features.** Some join your meetings as bots. Some run locally and don't. One is a physical device. They target different users, workflows, and privacy preferences. The wrong choice creates more friction than it eliminates.

This article tests all six tools across the criteria that actually matter: transcription accuracy, action item extraction, CRM integration, summary quality, and real-world usability. By the end, you'll know exactly which one belongs in your stack.

## What Are AI Meeting Note Tools?

AI meeting note tools automatically record, transcribe, summarize, and extract action items from meetings. Instead of scrambling to take notes while someone talks, these tools listen for you — then deliver clean notes, searchable transcripts, and to-do lists.

The market has exploded because remote and hybrid work is permanent. According to recent data, knowledge workers spend 35–50% of their week in meetings. Tools that capture and organize that time represent a major productivity unlock. Each tool in this comparison, however, takes a fundamentally different approach:

- **Bot-joiner tools** (Fireflies, Fathom, Read AI) deploy a virtual participant that joins your calendar meetings automatically.
- **Real-time captioning tools** (Otter) transcribe live during the meeting with a visible assistant.
- **Local-first tools** (Granola) run on your machine without any bot — no one knows it's recording.
- **Hardware tools** (Plaud) use a dedicated device you bring to any conversation.

These aren't just UX differences. They determine who can see the bot, whether recordings are allowed, and how deep your analytics go. Choose wrong, and you might find yourself in an awkward conversation about "why is there a bot in our client meeting?"

## Step-by-Step: How to Choose the Right AI Meeting Notes Tool for Your Workflow

Picking the wrong tool wastes time and money. Here's a systematic process to identify the one that fits your actual workflow.

### Step 1: Identify your meeting types
List the meetings you attend regularly. Are they mostly virtual (Zoom, Google Meet, Teams)? In-person? A mix? Do you host client calls where recording is sensitive? Do you need real-time captions for accessibility?

**Result:** This determines whether you need a bot-joiner, a local app, or a hardware device.

### Step 2: Determine your privacy threshold
If a bot joins your meeting, participants may see it depending on the tool. Ask yourself:
- Can you legally record your meetings? (Check your jurisdiction and company policy.)
- Would clients or guests object to a recording notice?
- Do you need to process audio locally for compliance reasons?

**Result:** If privacy is paramount, Granola (no bot, local processing) or Plaud (no network required during recording) are your options. If your team is fine with bots, Fireflies, Fathom, or Otter work.

### Step 3: Map your integrations
List the tools you use daily — your CRM (Salesforce, HubSpot), project management (Notion, Asana, Linear), Slack, and calendar. Cross-reference against each tool's integration support.

**Result:** Fireflies has the deepest CRM integration. Fathom and Otter cover the basics well. Granola and Plaud barely integrate. If CRM auto-logging saves you 10 minutes per call, a $19/mo tool pays for itself in the first week. [AFFILIATE: Fireflies.ai]

### Step 4: Evaluate free tier honestly
Sign up for the free tiers of your top 2–3 candidates. Run them through a real meeting week. Don't rely on marketing claims — test the summary quality, action item extraction, and search for yourself.

**Key test:** After 3 meetings, can you find a specific decision made 5 days ago? If no, that tool fails the core promise.

### Step 5: Check for the "bot problem"
If you're in a tool where the bot is visible (Fireflies, Fathom, Read AI), ask your team how they feel about it. Some teams adapt in a week. Others find it creates a surveillance culture. This is a people decision, not a tech decision.

**Workaround:** Fathom and Otter both offer recording modes that don't show a bot to other participants. Granola and Plaud sidestep the issue entirely.

## Best For / Worst For

### Fireflies.ai
**Best for:** Sales teams who need conversation intelligence (talk-to-listen ratio, objection detection) and deep CRM integration. The searchable meeting archive is unmatched.
**Worst for:** Privacy-conscious teams, light users who don't need analytics, or anyone who finds a bot in every meeting intrusive.

### Otter.ai
**Best for:** Individuals who want reliable real-time captioning, journalists conducting interviews, students recording lectures. The generous free tier makes it low-risk to start.
**Worst for:** Teams needing conversation analytics, power users in heavy CRM workflows, in-person meetings (requires mobile app).

### Fathom
**Best for:** Budget-conscious individuals and small teams. The unlimited free tier is a genuine competitive advantage. Clean interface, good summaries, and the manual recording mode avoids bot awkwardness.
**Worst for:** Enterprise teams needing deep analytics, CRM auto-logging on a budget (Pro tier required), or anyone who needs a mobile app. [AFFILIATE: Fathom]

### Granola
**Best for:** Privacy-sensitive professionals (legal, HR, executive), people who prefer an AI assistant rather than a fully automated note-taker. No bot, local processing.
**Worst for:** Anyone who wants "set it and forget it" automation. You must take notes actively. No mobile app, no CRM integrations.

### Read AI
**Best for:** Engineering managers and team leads who want to improve meeting culture. The meeting coach and health scores provide actionable data.
**Worst for:** Individual contributors, budget-conscious users. The analytics are overkill for people who just need clean notes.

### Plaud.ai
**Best for:** Journalists, interviewers, students, and anyone attending in-person meetings, lectures, or conferences. The hardware approach is uniquely suited for situations where you can't have a laptop open.
**Worst for:** Remote workers, virtual meeting automation. It doesn't integrate with Zoom or Teams and requires carrying a separate device.

## FAQ

### Which AI meeting notes tool has the best free tier?
Fathom. Its free tier offers unlimited meeting transcription and AI summaries for individual users — no time limits, no meeting caps. Otter's free tier is second-best at 300 minutes per month. Fireflies, Granola, and Read AI have restrictive free tiers that function more as trials.

### Can these tools transcribe in-person meetings?
Otter (via mobile app), Granola (desktop app), and Plaud (dedicated hardware device) can handle in-person meetings. Fireflies and Fathom are primarily designed for virtual meetings, though Fireflies supports uploaded audio files. Read AI requires a bot or browser extension and does not support in-person recording.

### Do meeting participants know a bot is recording?
It depends on the tool. Fireflies and Read AI place visible bots in the meeting (Fred and Riley, respectively). Fathom's bot is visible but less intrusive. Otter's Assistant joins without displaying a bot name. Granola and Plaud run entirely on-device with no visible presence — no one knows they're recording unless you tell them.

### Is Granola better than Fireflies for privacy?
Yes, significantly. Granola processes audio locally on your device — no cloud upload, no third-party servers. Fireflies uploads all audio to its cloud for processing. For meetings under NDA, involving sensitive client information, or in regulated industries, Granola is the safer choice. The trade-off is that Granola requires active note-taking and has almost no integrations.

### How do these tools handle multiple speakers?
Otter and Fireflies have the strongest speaker identification for meetings with 3–5 participants. Granola and Plaud (single-device) struggle with more than 2 speakers in a room. Read AI handles speaker identification well but can be inconsistent in larger groups. Fathom is solid for up to 6 participants. For large all-hands meetings, none of these tools are reliable for speaker attribution.

### Can I export my meeting notes to Notion or Obsidian?
Fireflies, Otter, and Fathom offer direct Notion integrations. Granola supports file export to your local machine. Plaud can share to Notion via the iOS share sheet but has no direct API. For Obsidian, most tools support Markdown export which Obsidian can import, though none offer native Obsidian integration yet. [LINK: AI note-taking app comparison]

## Conclusion: Which One Should You Use?

There is no single best tool. Here's the decision framework:

**If you're a sales team** that lives in Salesforce or HubSpot → **Fireflies**. The CRM auto-logging and conversation intelligence alone justify the cost.

**If you're a knowledge worker on a budget** who primarily attends virtual meetings → **Fathom**. The unlimited free tier is unmatched. Try it first — you may never need to upgrade.

**If you value privacy above all else** (legal, HR, executive) → **Granola**. No bot, local processing, no awkward conversations.

**If you're a manager** trying to improve your team's meeting culture → **Read AI**. The analytics and coaching features are unique.

**If you attend in-person meetings, lectures, or interviews** → **Plaud**. The hardware device works where nothing else does.

**If you want the most reliable real-time captions** for accessibility → **Otter**.

Start with Fathom's free tier. If it doesn't fit, upgrade to the tool that matches your primary workflow constraint — CRM, privacy, analytics, or device portability. The meeting notes you don't have to take today free up time for the work that actually matters.
